Becoming Aware of Mental Health Issues

Sophia Crennan, Justine Luo, Cynthia Xie





Education

Learn about mental health: stress, coping, and mental disorders.

*For educational purposes only! Please do not self diagnose. Seek professional help if you need it.*


Stress

  • Stress occurs when a situation overwhelms a person’s perceived ability to meet the demands of that situation
  • Stressors: events that trigger a stress response
  • Two major categories of stressors:
    • Major life events: any situation that creates a major upheaval in a person’s life might lead to stress
    • Daily hassles: accumulation of minor irritations wear people down mentally and physically
  • Effects:
    • Increases heart rate
    • Increases respiration
    • Raises blood pressure

Coping

  • Coping is the act of dealing with stress or emotions
  • Strategies:
    • Problem-focused coping: way of dealing with stress that aims to change the situation that is creating stress
      • Solve the problem
      • Seek social support
      • Take assertive action
    • Emotion-focused coping: way of dealing with stress that aims to regulate the experience of distress
      • Reappraise, distancing
      • Use escape-avoidance
      • Seek social support
      • Exercise self-control
      • Emotional disclosure
    • Social support: combines problem-focused and emotion-focused strategies
      • Develop social connectedness
      • Seek advice from or talk with friends and loved ones

Psychological Disorders

  • 3 criteria that behavior must be:
    • Deviant: different from the norm
    • Distressing: leads to real discomfort or anguish, either in the person directly or in others
    • Dysfunctional: interferes with everyday functioning and occasionally can be a risk to oneself or others
  • 26% of the population suffers from a diagnosable disorder at any given time
  • 46% of adults in the US will suffer from at least one psychological disorder over the course of their lifetime,
    many of which suffer from multiple disorders
    • The existence of two or more disorders at the same time is called comorbidity
  • Anxiety Disorders
    • Anxiety that is out of proportion to the situation and interferes with everyday functioning
    • Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
      • Symptoms: state of pervasive and excessive anxiety lasting at least 6 months
      • Behaviors: inability to relax
    • Panic disorder
      • Symptoms: persistent worry about having a panic attack
      • Panic attack: heart palpitations, trembling, dizziness, intense dread, and fear of dying
      • Panic disorder: prone to panic attacks, concerned about having a panic attack and about
        embarrassment of having a panic attack
    • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
      • Symptoms: anxiety disorder triggered by a traumatic experience
      • Behaviors: possible flashbacks, nightmares about the traumatic event, emotional numbness
        and avoidance of thoughts, feelings, and activities associated with the trauma,
        irritability or outbursts of anger, hypervigilance and trouble sleeping
    • Social phobia (social anxiety disorder)
      • Symptoms: persistent fear of humiliation in the presence of others
      • Behaviors: highly anxious, extremely self-conscious about
        appearance or behavior or both, possibly housebound
    • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD)
      • Symptoms: obsessive thoughts lead to compulsive behaviors
      • Behaviors: repetitive, uncontrollable urges or behaviors according to a ritualistic set of rules
      • Impulse control disorder: a person feels an intense, repetitive desire to perform certain behaviors
  • Mood Disorders
    • Disturbances in emotional behavior that prevent people from functioning effectively in everyday life
    • 2 major forms: depression, bipolar disorder
    • Major depressive disorder
      • Symptoms: pervasive low mood, lack of motivation, low energy, feelings of worthlessness
        and guilt that last for at least two consecutive weeks
      • Behaviors: change in eating behavior, intense anxiety or sadness,
        feeling of being disconnected, and/or inability to take pleasure in enjoyable experiences
    • Bipolar disorder
      • Symptoms: severe mood fluctuations, cycling between
        very low (depressive) and very high (manic) episodes
      • Manic episodes: increased energy, sleeplessness, euphoria, irritability,
        delusions of grandeur, increased sex drive, “racing” thoughts, indiscretion
  • Psychotic Disorders
    • Disorders of thought and perception
    • An inability to distinguish real from imagined perceptions
    • Schizophrenia
      • Profound disturbances in thought and emotion, specifically problems
        with perception, including hallucinations
      • Symptoms: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized
        speech, grossly disorganized behavior, or catatonic behavior
        (immobile and unresponsive, though awake), emotional flatness

Dissociative Disorders

  • Produce extreme splits or gaps in memory, identity, or consciousness
  • Dissociative identity disorder (DID)
    • A person develops at least two distinct personalities, each with its
      own memories, thoughts, behaviors, and emotions
    • Symptoms: amnesia, self-destructive behaviors, auditory hallucinations
    • Behaviors: not remembering anything about an experience or a particular
      period of their life, may cut themselves or attempt suicide, hear voices giving
      them orders that they feel compelled to obey
    • Cause: highly traumatic experience
  • Somatoform Disorders
    • Take bodily or physical form and mimic physical diseases but have no physical cause or medical basis
    • Somatization disorder
      • Person complains of multiple physical disorders that have no known medical or physical basis
      • Criteria:
        • A history of physical complaints prior to the age of 30 that occur over several years
        • Pain in at least 4 different sites on the body, such as the head, joints, or back
        • 2 gastrointestinal problems other than pain, such as vomiting or diarrhea
        • One sexual symptom, such as lack of interest or erectile dysfunction
        • One pseudoneurological symptom, such as fainting or blindness
    • Hypochondriasis
      • Pervasive and debilitating fear of suffering from serious physical illness although none is found by a medical professional
      • Behavior: misperceives, exaggerates, and becomes obsessively concerned with physical symptoms of a disease

Personality Disorders

  • Patterns of cognition, emotion, and behavior that develop in late childhood or adolescence and are maladaptive and inflexible
  • Schizoid personality disorder
    • Desire to avoid close relationships as well as by emotional aloofness, reclusivity, and a lack of humor
  • Schizotypal personality disorder
    • Desire to live an isolated and asocial life, but also by the presence of odd thoughts and beliefs
  • Paranoid personality disorder
    • Extreme suspicions and mistrust of others in unwarranted and maladaptive ways
  • Histrionic personality disorder
    • Desire to be the center of attention and by dramatic, seductive, flamboyant and exaggerated behaviors
  • Borderline personality disorder
    • Out-of-control emotions, fear of being abandoned by others, a vacillation between idealizing and despising
      people who are close to the person with the disorder
  • Narcissistic personality disorder
    • Having an extremely positive and arrogant self-image and being extraordinarily self-centered
    • Exaggerated sense of self-importance and grandiosity
  • Antisocial personality disorder
    • Extremely impulsive, deceptive, violent, ruthless, and callous behaviors
    • A serious and potentially dangerous disorder (45-75% of male prison inmates are diagnosed with this disorder)
  • Avoidant personality disorder
    • Extreme fear of being criticized, low self-esteem, avoidance of social interaction
  • Dependent personality disorder
    • Fear of being rejected and having a strong need to be cared for
  • Obsessive-compulsive personality disorder
    • Rigid habits and extreme perfectionism
    • More general than obsessive-compulsive disorder

Childhood Disorders

  • Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD)
    • Inability to focus attention for more than a few minutes, to remain still and quiet, to do careful work
  • Autistic syndrome disorder (autism)
    • Severe language and social impairment along with repetitive habits and inward-focused behaviors
  • Asperger’s syndrome
    • Impaired social interest and skills and restricted interests
    • Intelligence is usually above average and language is not delayed or deficient
